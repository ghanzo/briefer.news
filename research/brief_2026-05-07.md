# State of the Board — May 7, 2026

Cuba sanctions framework lands in the Federal Register as the Justice Department opens enforcement fronts against UCLA, Colorado, and a major New York hospital system; State and Treasury keep escalating Iran's oil-trade pressure.

---

## Significant items

- **Cuba sanctions framework.** Executive Order 14404, signed May 1 and published May 7, builds on January's national-emergency declaration (EO 14380) to sanction Cuban government officials and any persons operating in Cuba's energy, defense, metals and mining, financial services, or security sectors. The order also reaches owners and controllers of designated entities, persons providing material or financial support, persons responsible for serious human rights abuse or corruption in Cuba, and — notably — adult family members of designated persons. Foreign financial institutions transacting for designated persons can have their U.S. correspondent accounts cut off. Authorities: IEEPA, NEA, INA §212(f).[^1]

- **Treasury sanctions program maintenance.** OFAC published seven sanctions general license updates on May 7 covering Russia (GLs 55E, 115C, 13P, 131C), Iran, Venezuela (GLs 46–46B and 47–50), the Democratic Republic of the Congo, Belarus, and the global terrorism / illicit drug trade programs.[^2]

- **Iran-China oil sanctions.** State and Treasury sanctioned Qingdao Haiye Oil Terminal Co., Ltd. — a China-based petroleum terminal that has imported "tens of millions of barrels" of sanctioned Iranian crude since the issuance of National Security Presidential Memorandum 2 — alongside three Iranian currency exchange houses that process billions of dollars annually, converting illicit oil revenue into usable currency. State acted under E.O. 13846; Treasury under E.O. 13902. The action is framed by State as part of "Economic Fury," its named maximum-pressure campaign.[^3]

- **National emergency continuations.** Presidential continuations of national emergencies regarding Iraq stabilization and the Central African Republic were published May 7.[^4]

- **DOJ v. UCLA Medical School.** A year-long Civil Rights Division investigation determined that UCLA's David Geffen School of Medicine intentionally selected applicants based on race. Documents reviewed reportedly indicate UCLA leadership held the view that "patients receive the best care when treated by a doctor of the same race." Investigation found admitted Black and Hispanic applicants had, on average, "consistently lower academic qualifications than their white and Asian counterparts."[^5]

- **DOJ v. New York-Presbyterian Hospital.** The Antitrust Division and U.S. Attorney's Office for the Southern District of New York filed a Sherman Act §1 suit against New York-Presbyterian — the largest hospital system in New York City, with eight hospitals and many outpatient facilities. The complaint alleges contract restrictions that prevent insurers from offering plans that exclude New York-Presbyterian or place rivals in better tiers, and forbid lower copays at competing hospitals. The Division called this its "second case this year" challenging healthcare market structure.[^6]

- **DOJ v. Colorado.** A federal suit filed against the State of Colorado challenges its standard-capacity firearms-magazine ban as unconstitutional. The complaint argues the law bans magazines that come standard with AR-15-style rifles, citing District of Columbia v. Heller (2008). Prosecuted by the Civil Rights Division's new Second Amendment Section under AAG Harmeet Dhillon.[^7]

- **DPRK IT-worker enablement.** Two U.S. nationals — Matthew Issac Knoot (Nashville) and Erick Ntekereze Prince (New York) — were each sentenced to 18 months for hosting laptop farms that allowed North Korean IT workers to fraudulently obtain remote employment with U.S. companies. The schemes generated more than $1.2 million for the DPRK and impacted nearly 70 U.S. companies. Prince used his company Taggcar Inc. to fraudulently supply "certified" IT workers; Knoot's case began with an FBI search of his Nashville residence in August 2023. Part of the joint NSD/FBI "DPRK RevGen: Domestic Enabler Initiative."[^8]

- **CISA KEV addition.** CISA added CVE-2026-0300 — a Palo Alto Networks PAN-OS out-of-bounds write vulnerability with active exploitation evidence — to the Known Exploited Vulnerabilities catalog. Federal civilian agencies are required to remediate per Binding Operational Directive 22-01.[^9]

- **Defense contracting rules.** Two final DFARS rules were published May 7. DFARS Case 2021-D011 establishes requirements to mitigate risks related to foreign ownership, control, or influence in defense contracts. DFARS Case 2024-D021 establishes greenhouse gas emissions disclosure requirements for defense contractors.[^10]

- **FCC wireline broadband rule.** FCC final rule published May 7, "Accelerating Wireline Broadband Deployment by Removing Barriers to Infrastructure Investment."[^11]

- **NESE Pipeline groundbreaking.** Williams Companies broke ground April 14 on the Northeast Supply Enhancement Pipeline — an extension of the Transco system across Pennsylvania, New Jersey, and New York that will add 400,000 dekatherms per day of natural-gas capacity (equivalent to 2.3 million homes) into New York City and Long Island. In service Q4 2027. Interior projects $6 billion in electricity-bill savings over 15 years and $1.8 billion in economic development. Energy Sec. Wright, EPA Adm. Zeldin, and Interior Sec. Burgum attended; Williams CEO Chad Zamarin called it a $1 billion direct investment.[^12]

- **Trump Peace Pipelines Framework.** At the Three Seas Initiative Summit in Dubrovnik, Croatia on April 28, Energy Sec. Wright launched a U.S.-led MOU to expand natural-gas infrastructure across Central and Eastern Europe and accelerate American LNG exports to the region. Concurrent deals included a U.S.-Croatia civil-nuclear cooperation joint statement (small modular reactor deployment under the FIRST program), a Bosnia-Herzegovina–Croatia–U.S. industry framework for the Southern Interconnection Gas Pipeline, and **Project Pantheon** — an American firm's $50+ billion next-generation data center in Croatia.[^13]

- **DRC–M23 peace process.** Joint statement issued April 18 reporting that representatives of the DRC government and the Alliance Fleuve Congo / March 23 Movement (AFC/M23) — together with Qatar, the United States, Togo (as African Union mediator), the African Union Commission, and Switzerland — met April 13–17 in Montreux to advance the Doha Framework signed November 15, 2025. Topics included humanitarian access, judicial protection, ceasefire oversight, and prisoner release.[^14]

- **Pax Silica.** Norway joined the U.S.-led Pax Silica Initiative on May 6, becoming the 15th signatory alongside Australia, Finland, India, Israel, Japan, the Philippines, Qatar, the Republic of Korea, Singapore, Sweden, the UAE, the UK, and the U.S. Norwegian Ambassador to the United States Anniken Huitfeldt signed the declaration in Washington. The initiative coordinates diversified critical-mineral supply chains.[^15]

- **MFN drug pricing.** The White House published an analysis of its Most-Favored-Nation drug-pricing policy. Seventeen of the largest pharmaceutical manufacturers have signed voluntary MFN agreements. The prospective MFN framework — under which new U.S. drug launches must price comparably to other high-income countries — projects $529 billion in domestic savings over ten years; existing-drug provisions for state Medicaid programs project $64.3 billion in federal/state savings. The TrumpRx.gov direct-to-consumer channel is forecast to save uninsured GLP-1 users about $3,000/year and IVF couples more than $6,000.[^16]

## Voice tracker

> "The policies, practices, and actions of the Government of Cuba … are repugnant to the moral and political values of free and democratic societies." — Executive Order 14404, *Imposing Sanctions on Those Responsible for Repression in Cuba*, May 1.[^1]

> "UCLA's admissions process has been focused on racial demographics at the expense of merit and excellence — allowing racial politics to distract the school from the vital work of training great doctors. Racism in admissions is both illegal and anti-American, and this Department will not allow it to continue." — Assistant Attorney General Harmeet K. Dhillon, Civil Rights Division, on the UCLA Medical School determination, May 6.[^5]

> "America is producing as much natural gas as Russia, China and Iran combined. The U.S. also leads the world in LNG exports and is on track to more than double its exports within the next decade. President Trump is unleashing a new era of cooperation for Central and Eastern Europe." — Energy Secretary Chris Wright at the Three Seas Initiative Summit, Dubrovnik, on the launch of the Trump Peace Pipelines Framework, April 28.[^13]

> "These sentences hold accountable U.S. nationals who enabled North Korea's illicit efforts to infiltrate U.S. networks and profit on the back of U.S. companies. The National Security Division will continue to pursue those who, through deception and cyber-enabled fraud, threaten our national security." — Assistant Attorney General John A. Eisenberg, National Security Division, on the Knoot and Prince sentencings, May 6.[^8]
