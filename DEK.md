# DEK.md — The Day's Narrative voice

The dek is the one paragraph that does the most editorial work in the brief. The headline names the day's biggest fact; the dek names the day's **shape**. Most readers will read the headline, the dek, and the first bullet — then either keep reading or close the tab. The dek decides which.

This document is binding. Synth must follow every rule below.

---

## What the dek is for

The dek is the **one line the brief is willing to be wrong about** — the editor's stance on what the day *means*, not a recap of what happened. A reader who skims only the dek should walk away with a *posture*, not a summary.

Three working properties:

1. **Stance over scope.** Pick a posture and let the items support it. Who's pressed today, who's improvising, what got harder, what was choreographed. A good dek can be argued with.
2. **One sentence does the work; the second adds the wrinkle.** Not "thesis + list," but "thesis + tension." The wrinkle is where the brief's intelligence shows.
3. **A specific, concrete observation beats a doctrine label.** The dek is reportage, not policy analysis. Doctrine names (15FYP, NQPF, dual circulation, etc.) belong in the Strategic Backdrop cards, not in the dek.

---

## Hard rules

- **Length: 30–55 words. Two sentences maximum.** A dek over 55 words is hiding indecision. A single sentence is allowed if it carries.
- **No "X threads through today" / "X sits behind every move today" / "Beijing meets [event] from a posture of [Y]" openers.** These are templates. Synth has reused them word-for-word across consecutive days — that's a tell that the model is filling a slot, not thinking. Rotate openers; never reuse a previous day's opening clause.
- **No item enumeration.** Comma-chain construction — "the X, the Y, and the Z [verb] W" — is banned. Pick a stance and let two of today's items support it implicitly. If you find yourself naming three bullets, you're writing a TL;DR, not a dek.
- **No doctrine name-drop in the dek.** Do not write "new quality productive forces," "15th Five-Year Plan," "common prosperity," "dual circulation," "30/60 carbon," "Made in China 2025" inside the dek. Doctrine framing is the Strategic Backdrop card's job. The dek anchors to *the day's specific human moment*, not the doctrinal label behind it.
- **No "while/even as/even" hedge tic at the end.** The brief has fallen into a habit of ending every dek with a contrast clause. Use it once a week if it's earned; otherwise the dek's second sentence should *commit*, not hedge.
- **No claim that isn't supported by the 9 bullets.** The dek's STANCE is editorial; its FACTS must be in the bullets.

---

## What the dek must do

A dek that ships should satisfy at least three of these five:

1. **Name a posture** — who is pressed, who is improvising, what was choreographed, what was conceded. ("Washington walked in with leverage on paper and Taiwan unmentioned on the U.S. side; Xi mentioned it first.")
2. **Notice an asymmetry** — between what's said vs. done, who moved first vs. who answered, what's loud vs. what's quietly significant. ("The cameras caught the warmth. The harder sentence sat one paragraph deeper in the Chinese readout.")
3. **Make a small, falsifiable claim** — something a careful reader could disagree with. ("Today's Iran moves are coalition theater for a deal that's already underway.")
4. **Pair an unexpected couple** — name two items the reader wouldn't have grouped together, and explain the link. ("A new AI rulebook and a 14-bill 2026 lawmaking plan look like routine regulatory drift; both are 15FYP groundwork no one has had to say out loud yet." — note: doctrine reference is in a *causal* clause, not the dek's lead claim; this is borderline OK but should be avoided when possible.)
5. **Anchor a thread that returns tomorrow** — flag the day's move as part of an arc the reader can pick up the next morning. ("Day 76 of the Iran war ran through every announcement; tomorrow's question is whether the Paris communiqué carries Berlin.")

If a draft dek does fewer than three of these, rewrite it.

## Worked examples — what we've shipped vs. what we should ship

### China · 2026-05-14

**Shipped:**

> Beijing meets Trump's state visit from a posture of internal consolidation. Xi names Taiwan the first red line and brands ties "constructive strategic stable," while at home a new AI rulebook and 2026 lawmaking plan groundwork the 15th Five-Year Plan.

**What's wrong:** Banned opener (Beijing meets... posture of...). Doctrine name-drop (15FYP). Hedge tic ("while at home..."). Three-item enumeration. Zero falsifiable claim — the reader cannot argue with this dek.

**Stronger:**

> Trump came to Beijing for the trade-deal photo; Xi made him hear the Taiwan line first. The economic teams produced "balanced outcomes," the cameras got their warmth, and the one sentence the readouts had to carry was the harder one.

Why it works: names a posture (Xi set the agenda), notices an asymmetry (photo vs. red-line moment), makes a falsifiable claim (was Xi the one who set the agenda? — arguable). Doctrine reference: absent. Length: 47 words. Two sentences.

### China · 2026-05-13

**Shipped:**

> Beijing meets Trump's state visit from a posture of internal consolidation. The day's AI rulebook and 14-bill lawmaking slate advance Xi's "new quality productive forces" doctrine, while a new carbon-assessment regime locks 30/60 emissions targets into cadre evaluation.

**What's wrong:** Identical opener to 2026-05-14 (banned reuse). Two doctrine name-drops (NQPF + 30/60). Three-item enumeration in sentence two. Reads as bullet recap with a thesis label slapped on.

**Stronger:**

> The day before Trump lands, Beijing publishes the rulebooks. A first national framework for AI agents and a carbon-tied performance review for officials are the kind of moves a regime makes when it wants the visiting president to find the room already arranged.

Why it works: posture (Beijing pre-arranges the stage). Falsifiable (was this timing deliberate? — arguable). Pairs two items the reader wouldn't have grouped (AI agents + cadre reviews). Doctrine: absent. Length: 49 words.

### US · 2026-05-14

**Shipped:**

> Iran sits behind nearly every move today. Washington flies to Xi with Hormuz on the agenda, the UK contributes drones and warships to a forty-nation patrol, and a presidential determination keeps the squeeze on Iran-oil buyers alive.

**What's wrong:** Banned opener ("Iran sits behind..."). Three-item enumeration. No falsifiable claim. The reader learns nothing they wouldn't learn from the bullets in 30 seconds.

**Stronger:**

> Washington is asking Beijing to help close the Strait of Hormuz on the same day forty other capitals say they will. The summit's economic agenda is real; the energy of the day is the coalition agreeing the Iran war has a near horizon.

Why it works: posture (coalition convergence on near-term resolution). Falsifiable (is the war near a horizon? — strong claim, argue with it). Anchors a thread (tomorrow we can ask whether the horizon held). Length: 50 words. No bullet-by-bullet recap.

### US · 2026-05-13

**Shipped:**

> Iran's war runs through almost everything today. Forty navies organize around the Strait of Hormuz, fresh sanctions hit the IRGC's oil network, and the Strategic Petroleum Reserve sends another tranche toward market — even the Navy's new shipbuilding plan reads like a hedge.

**What's wrong:** Banned opener. Three-item enumeration. The "even the Navy's shipbuilding plan reads like a hedge" clause is actually doing real editorial work — it's the *only* sentence in any recent dek that pairs an unexpected couple and makes a falsifiable claim. The rest of the dek buries it under a recap.

**Stronger:**

> The Navy's new 30-year shipbuilding plan reads like a hedge against the war it is currently fighting. Forty capitals coordinated on Hormuz today; the Pentagon's longest planning horizon, published the same morning, assumes the war doesn't end soon.

Why it works: takes what was the dek's best clause and makes it the lead. Posture (Pentagon planning to outlast the war). Falsifiable (is the shipbuilding plan a hedge? — interpretive, arguable). Pairs an unexpected couple (Hormuz coalition + 30-year shipbuilding plan). Length: 44 words.

## When to break these rules

The rules above are binding for the typical day. Three exceptions:

1. **A single concrete event is so dominant that any framing reads as evasion.** A presidential resignation, a major military strike, a market crash, a Politburo-tier purge. On those days, the dek can simply name the event with weight and one wrinkle. ("The defense minister fell at 4 a.m. The Party gave it three hundred words.")
2. **A single quote does the work better than any synthesis.** If a head-of-state line, a court ruling, or a sanctions order is the day's most quotable text, the dek can be built around it. ("Xi: 'Taiwan independence and cross-Strait peace are as irreconcilable as fire and water.' The U.S. side, on the record, said nothing.")
3. **The week itself is the story.** Once or twice a month, the dek can step back: "Three Fridays in a row, the Federal Reserve has hinted and not moved." Use sparingly; the brief is daily, not weekly.

---

## A short checklist for the synth before writing the dek

Before drafting, answer:

- What is the *posture* I'm naming? (Not what happened — who's pressed, improvising, choreographing.)
- What is the wrinkle I add in sentence two? (Asymmetry, falsifiable claim, unexpected pairing, thread anchor.)
- Have I used a banned opener? Have I name-dropped a doctrine? Have I listed three items?
- Could a careful reader disagree with this?

If the answer to the last question is no, the dek is too safe. Rewrite.
